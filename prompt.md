You are helping a computational linguist and natural language professor design an 18-week course that uses Python's Natural Language Toolkit (NLTK) to analyze James Joyce's Ulysses. The professor has a background in formal and computational linguistics, a deep appreciation for the strange and the absurd, and wants the course to feel like an intellectual adventure — not a dry methods survey.

The core conceit: Each of the 18 episodes of Ulysses maps onto a different NLTK capability, submodule, technique, or conceptual domain within NLP. The pairings should not be arbitrary. Joyce assigned each episode its own literary technique (from the Linati and Gilbert schemas — narrative, monologue, catechism, hallucination, fuga per canonem, gigantism, etc.), its own art (theology, history, philology, music, science, etc.), and its own bodily organ. Use these schemas, and the actual stylistic character of each episode, to make pairings that are intellectually motivated. When a chapter is written as a musical fugue, that should connect to something about sequential structure or pattern detection; when a chapter is structured as Q&A catechism, that should connect to something about information extraction or structured parsing. The mapping should reward students who actually read the novel.

For each episode/week, provide:

Episode title (Homeric name) and a one-sentence characterization of its literary style/technique (drawn from Joyce's schemas and the critical tradition).

NLTK focus area — the specific tool, submodule, method, or conceptual domain being introduced that week.

The pairing rationale — a short paragraph (2–4 sentences) explaining why this episode and this NLTK topic belong together. This should be genuinely thoughtful, not hand-wavy. The professor will use this rationale in lecture to motivate the week's work.

Core exercises — 2–3 concrete tasks students would perform on the episode's text using the week's NLTK tools. These should be real, executable ideas, not vague gestures.

Diving deeper — 3–5 bullet points connecting the week's work to more advanced or adjacent topics (other libraries like spaCy or gensim, research papers, unsolved problems in computational stylistics, connections to later weeks in the course). These should give ambitious students somewhere to go and give the professor material for seminar-style discussion.

Progression: The course should move from foundational techniques (tokenization, frequency analysis) in the early weeks toward more sophisticated methods (classification, language modeling, semantic analysis) in the later weeks. This mirrors the arc of Ulysses itself, which begins with relatively conventional narration and grows progressively more experimental.

Tone: The professor wants the syllabus to have personality. Episode descriptions and pairing rationales can be wry, allusive, and playful — this is a course about Joyce, after all. Avoid boilerplate academic language. If something is genuinely weird or funny about a pairing, say so.

Deliver the plan three episodes at a time, so we can review and adjust before continuing. Start with Episodes 1–3 (Telemachus, Nestor, Proteus).
