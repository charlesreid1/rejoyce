# Week 6: Hades
### *"How grand we are this morning" — A funeral, a carriage ride, and the longest catalog of the dead in modern fiction.*

Bloom rides in a funeral carriage to Glasnevin Cemetery for Paddy Dignam's burial, accompanied by Simon Dedalus, Martin Cunningham, and Mr. Power. The episode is a tour through death's territory — they pass the site of Childs' murder case, the Mater Misericordiae hospital, the statue of Sir Philip Crampton, Paddy's house, and finally the cemetery itself with its catalog of graves, crosses, and remembered dead. The episode's art is *religion*; its organ is the *heart*; its technique is *incubism* (haunting, the pressure of the dead upon the living). Bloom thinks of his dead father, his dead son Rudy, his own mortality — but also of practical matters: the cost of coffins, the chemistry of decomposition, the business model of cemeteries. The tonal register is extraordinary: grief, gallows humor, banality, and tenderness coexist sentence by sentence.

**NLTK Focus:** Sentiment analysis and affective lexicons (`nltk.sentiment`, VADER, SentiWordNet, opinion lexicons, and their limitations)

**Pairing Rationale:**
A funeral is the paradigmatic sentiment-analysis problem: a scene that should, by every naive heuristic, score as uniformly negative. And yet Hades is one of the most tonally complex episodes in the novel. Bloom's response to death is not grief-as-expected but a rich mixture of empathy, dark humor, annoyance, scientific curiosity, and genuine sorrow — often within a single paragraph. This makes Hades a superb diagnostic for sentiment analysis tools, which tend to perform well on text with strong, consistent polarity (product reviews, political speech) and poorly on text where tone is mixed, ironic, or understated. Students will apply VADER and SentiWordNet to the episode and discover that the tools flatten precisely what makes the writing remarkable. The gap between the tool's output and the reader's experience is itself the lesson.

**Core Exercises:**

1. **Sentiment trajectory.** Divide Hades into roughly equal segments (e.g., by paragraph or by 50-sentence windows). Apply VADER's `polarity_scores` to each segment and plot the compound sentiment score across the episode. Annotate the plot with key narrative events (passing the Childs murder house, arriving at the cemetery, the burial itself, Bloom's memory of Rudy). Does the sentiment trajectory track the narrative arc? Where does VADER capture real emotional shifts, and where does it miss entirely? Identify at least three passages where VADER's score feels clearly wrong and explain why.

2. **Bloom's stoicism vs. the narrator's register.** Separate (approximately) Bloom's interior thoughts from the external narration and dialogue. Apply VADER to each stream independently. Hypothesis: Bloom's interior monologue is more tonally variable and harder for VADER to score accurately than the external narration, because Bloom's relationship to death is idiosyncratic while the narrator's descriptions hew closer to conventional sentiment cues. Test this. What features of Bloom's internal language (short fragments, questions, dark jokes, practical observations) confuse the sentiment model?

3. **Building a death lexicon.** SentiWordNet assigns positive, negative, and objective scores to WordNet synsets. Look up 20 death-related words from Hades (*coffin*, *cemetery*, *grave*, *corpse*, *funeral*, *mourning*, *burial*, *decay*, etc.) in SentiWordNet. Are their sentiment scores sensible? Now look up words that Bloom uses in *proximity* to death but that carry different associations in other contexts (*warm*, *quiet*, *home*, *garden*, *rest*). How well does a context-free sentiment lexicon handle words whose emotional valence depends entirely on their surroundings? Write a short reflection on what a "correct" sentiment analysis of a funeral chapter would even mean.

**Diving Deeper:**

- VADER was designed for social media text and uses a hand-crafted, empirically validated lexicon. Its assumptions (capitalization = emphasis, exclamation marks = intensity, "but" signals polarity shift) encode a model of how *internet users* express sentiment. How well do these assumptions transfer to early-twentieth-century literary prose? Enumerate specific VADER heuristics and test whether they help or hurt on Joyce.
- Irony and sarcasm are among the hardest problems in sentiment analysis. Hades contains both — Bloom's interior responses to the funeral's pomposities are frequently ironic. Explore recent work on irony detection: Joshi et al. (2017), Van Hee et al. (2018). Could you train a simple irony classifier on annotated data and apply it to Bloom's thoughts?
- Emotion detection goes beyond positive/negative polarity to identify specific emotions (anger, sadness, joy, fear, disgust, surprise). Plutchik's wheel of emotions provides one taxonomy; the NRC Emotion Lexicon (`nrc` via `nltk.sentiment`) provides a computational implementation. Apply the NRC lexicon to Hades and generate an emotion profile for the episode. Is *sadness* dominant, or does something else surface?
- The concept of *appraisal* in linguistics (Martin & White, 2005) offers a much richer framework for analyzing evaluative language than simple sentiment polarity. Appraisal theory distinguishes *affect* (emotional response), *judgement* (ethical evaluation), and *appreciation* (aesthetic evaluation). Bloom's funeral thoughts move fluidly among all three. How would you computationally implement even a crude appraisal analysis?
- Connection to Week 15 (Circe): that hallucinatory episode will resurface many of the emotional themes from Hades — the dead return, guilt is dramatized, grief is performed. The sentiment tools' limitations discovered here will frame the much harder problem of analyzing emotional content in surrealist, non-realist text.
