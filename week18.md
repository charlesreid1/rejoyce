# Week 18: Penelope
### *"...yes I said yes I will Yes." — Eight sentences, no punctuation, and the hidden structure of an apparently formless mind.*

The novel ends in Molly Bloom's bed. She lies beside Leopold, half-asleep, and her mind moves. Penelope is eight enormous "sentences" — unpunctuated, unparagraphed, flowing continuously for tens of thousands of words. There are no periods (two were added in error by typesetters and have persisted in some editions; Joyce intended none). There are no question marks, no quotation marks, no dashes, no colons. There are commas — or rather, there is the word *and* and the word *yes* and the word *then*, which serve as the only structural connectives in a monologue that ranges across Molly's girlhood in Gibraltar, her lovers, her marriage, her body, her opinions on men, her opinions on women, her memory of Bloom's proposal on Howth Head, and the great affirmation that closes the book. The technique is *monologue (female)*; the art is listed as *none* or *all* (the episode transcends the schema); the organ is *flesh*. There is no Homeric correspondence for the organ because Penelope is not the body — she is the body whole, the tissue and current that the skeleton of Ithaca could not represent.

**NLTK Focus:** Unsupervised text segmentation, topic modeling, and the discovery of latent structure (`nltk.tokenize.texttiling`, `gensim.models.LdaModel`, topic coherence, text segmentation without punctuation, the return to tokenization)

**Pairing Rationale:**
The course began in Week 1 with tokenization — the act of finding boundaries in a stream of language. Penelope returns to that primal problem and strips away every aid. There are no sentence boundaries. There are no paragraph boundaries. There are no typographic signals of any kind. The student is left with the raw stream and the question: *is there structure here, or is it really formless?*

The answer — and this is the secret that computational methods can reveal — is that Penelope is deeply structured, but its structure is thematic rather than syntactic. Molly's monologue cycles through recurring topics (Bloom, Boylan, Gibraltar, her body, her singing career, her opinions on other women) in a pattern that is not random but not linear either. Topic modeling and text segmentation algorithms can detect these latent thematic shifts without relying on punctuation — they find the seams in the fabric by tracking where the vocabulary changes. The result is a map of Molly's mind: not the neat hierarchy of a parse tree or the explicit taxonomy of a catechism, but a fluid, overlapping, recursive topography of attention.

The circularity is the point. Week 1 asked *where do words begin?* Week 18 asks *where do thoughts begin?* They are the same question, asked at different scales. The tools have grown enormously sophisticated — from `word_tokenize` to LDA — but the fundamental problem has not changed: segmentation, the drawing of boundaries in continuous signal. Molly's "yes" is the word that refuses to be bounded, that connects everything to everything else. The course ends where the novel ends: with the discovery that the boundaries were always provisional.

**Core Exercises:**

1. **Segment the unsegmentable.** Apply NLTK's TextTiling algorithm (`TextTilingTokenizer`) to Penelope, treating the raw text (without punctuation) as input. TextTiling detects topic shifts by measuring vocabulary change in sliding windows — it was designed for expository text, and Molly's monologue will challenge it. How many segments does it find? Where does it place the boundaries? Compare the algorithmic segmentation to the eight "sentences" that Joyce defined (the episode's only explicit structural divisions). Do the topic boundaries align with Joyce's sentence boundaries, or does the algorithm find a different hidden structure? Visualize both segmentations as a timeline of the episode, with Joyce's divisions on one track and TextTiling's on another.

2. **Topic modeling Molly's mind.** Divide Penelope into fixed-length windows (e.g., 200 tokens each) and run Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA via `gensim`) to discover latent topics. Experiment with different numbers of topics (k=4, 6, 8, 10). For each k, examine the top words per topic and assign interpretive labels (e.g., "Gibraltar/girlhood," "Bloom/marriage," "Boylan/desire," "body/physicality," "other women/judgment"). Plot the topic distribution across the episode as a **stacked area chart** — a flowing, multi-colored stream showing how Molly's attention shifts between topics. Does the visualization reveal a pattern? Critics have argued that Penelope follows a roughly cyclical structure, with certain themes recurring at regular intervals. Does your topic model confirm this? Identify the moments of strongest topic shift — the points where Molly's mind makes its sharpest turns.

3. **The return to tokenization.** Penelope defeats sentence tokenization — `sent_tokenize` will either treat the entire episode as one sentence or fragment it arbitrarily at commas. But word tokenization still works, and with it, all the tools from Week 1. Compute the same basic statistics you computed for Telemachus in Week 1: total tokens, total types, type-token ratio, average "sentence" length (using your segmentation from Exercise 1 as the sentence proxy), top 50 words with and without stopwords. Compare the two episodes. Telemachus and Penelope are the novel's bookends — the first and last voices the reader encounters. Are they linguistically similar or different? Build a **parallel profile**: two columns, same metrics, Telemachus on the left, Penelope on the right. The comparison should reveal something about the novel's arc: from a structured, conventionally punctuated, allusion-dense, multi-voiced opening to a structureless, unpunctuated, body-centered, single-voiced close. The metrics are the skeleton of that journey.

**Diving Deeper:**

- Bayesian segmentation methods (Eisenstein & Barzilay, 2008) and neural topic segmentation (Koshorek et al., 2018) outperform TextTiling on most benchmarks. Apply one or both to Penelope and compare. Does a more powerful segmentation algorithm find structure that TextTiling misses? Or is Penelope genuinely more resistant to segmentation than expository text?
- Molly's monologue has been analyzed through the lens of écriture féminine (Cixous, 1976) — writing that resists patriarchal structure, that flows rather than categorizes. Your topic model imposes structure on this flow. Is that an act of analytical violence — forcing Molly's voice into boxes — or does it reveal a structure that was always there, invisible to linear reading? This is not a technical question, but it is the most important question the course can ask about the relationship between computation and literature.
- The word *yes* appears approximately 90 times in Penelope. Track its distribution. Is it evenly spread or clustered? Does it mark topic shifts, emotional peaks, or something else? Compare the distribution of *yes* to the distribution of *no* and *and*. These three words are the structural particles of the episode — the connective tissue. Their distribution patterns are the closest thing Penelope has to punctuation.
- Dynamic Topic Models (Blei & Lafferty, 2006) extend LDA by modeling topic evolution over time. Apply a DTM to the full novel (one "time slice" per episode) and watch how topics emerge, evolve, and dissolve across the 18 weeks. Penelope should show topics that have been latent for many episodes suddenly resurfacing — Molly has been thinking offstage all day. Does the model capture this resurgence?
- Connection to Week 1 (Telemachus) and to every other week: Penelope is the final exam, the episode that touches everything. Its lack of punctuation tests tokenization. Its stream-of-consciousness flow tests POS tagging and parsing. Its single, sustained voice tests stylometric tools. Its emotional complexity tests sentiment analysis. Its thematic recursion tests topic modeling. Its relationship to every prior episode tests entity extraction and network analysis. If you have built the tools well, Penelope is where you discover what they can and cannot see. The course ends not with mastery but with honest reckoning — which is, not coincidentally, how the novel ends too.
